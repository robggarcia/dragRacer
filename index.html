Goal

The goal of this project was to design, program, and build a drag racing vehicle. The vehicle was designed according to design specifications outlined in the Project Assignment page found on our classroom Canvas page. The goal of the vehicle was to race from a standstill across a 10 foot track and cross the finish line in the fastest time. Gears are necessary for a competitive design in order to increase the speed of the provided TT motors.

Planning

I considered several different designs during the planning phase of my project. This included different gear ratios, orientations, and layouts. Although I had the option to use two TT motors, I wanted to constrain my design to just using one in order to minimize potential issues. I initially wanted to use helical gears in order to have my motor and rear axle spinning 90 degrees relative to each other.

As I began creating my CAD model, I soon realized that this would be a very difficult task. As an alternative, I decided to design spur gears with teeth at 45 degrees. This would allow me to achieve a very similar effect to my original idea, while still being able to take advantage of available feature scripts in Onshape.

Another design change that occurred during the planning phase involved what gear ratio would be used. I initially believed that I could achieve a 10:1 gear ratio, however as I investigated this further I discovered the TT motor would not have enough torque to spin the axle. Therefore, in order to ensure the successful completion of my drag racer, I chose a more conservative gear ratio of 4:1.

Here are some additional highlights from the planning phase of the design process.

  • Drive Style: Two wheel rear drive. Adjustable interface between Rear Axle and Drive Gear.
  • Wheelbase: Four wheel design, including a free spinning front axle with two laser cut acrylic wheels.
  • Gear Train: A single gear train made up of two gears spinning 90 degrees from each other.
  • Gear Ratio: Final design includes a 4:1 ratio.
  • Race Date: 5/27/2022. This date was firm. If I missed it, I would not be able to compete.

Wiring

Code

This program was written on the Arduino IDE. There were several sequences that were required to occur before the race car would be allowed to start. At the starting gate, the car would be placed on the ground and the photointerrupter would be blocked. With the photointerrupter blocked, I would then be able to press a button to arm the car. This button would not work unless the photointerrupter was blocked. Then, once the photointerrupter was opened up, the Arduino would send a signal to the H-Bridge module to run both motors. The time that the motors run is adjustable from a trim-pot that I installed in the prototyping shield.

Reflection

This vehicle was created to be used as an example for students and fulfilled all of the project requirements. It successfully raced down the track, with a fastest recorded time of 4.32 seconds. The code allowed the racer to progress through each step of the race, including turning on the vehicle, placing it in the starting gate, arming the vehicle, and racing. I encountered several problems along the way that required additional thought or design changes.

I initially designed my gear ratio to be 10:1. This would result in a top speed of just over 6 meters/second. When I tested this design, I learned that the TT motors do not have enough torque to start the vehicle moving at a 10:1 ratio. This required a redesign down to a more conservative value of a 4:1 gear ratio. This updated design worked well, but required significant updates to the CAD model. With the design changes in place, the top speed of the vehicle dropped from 5 meters/second to just over 3 meters/second. Friction played a major role as well. Sanding the surfaces where the 3D printed components meet such as between the axle and bearing blocks improved performance.

Overall I was very satisfied with my drag racer. Although it does not go as fast as I initially hoped for, my design serves as a good starting reference for students. If I were to do the project over again, I would focus on designing a gearbox assembly that uses additional gears and using simple laser cut acrylic spur gears that all rotate in the same plane. These changes would result in a much faster racer that would be quicker to design and build.
